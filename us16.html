EARTH’S FIRST SNAPSHOT FROM SPACE (1946)

On October 24, 1946, humans captured the very first photograph of Earth from space. Taken by a V-2 rocket launched from White Sands Missile Range in New Mexico, this historic image provided humanity's first perspective of our planet as seen from beyond the Earth's atmosphere.

Clyde Holliday, the engineer who developed the camera system, noted in National Geographic that the photograph showed "how our Earth would look to visitors from another planet coming in on a spaceship," forever altering our understanding of our home planet and our place in the universe.

This milestone was foundational for the future of space photography and Earth observation, demonstrating the immense scientific and symbolic value of viewing Earth from space.

Specifications and Facts:

Date Captured:October 24, 1946
Location:White Sands Missile Range, New Mexico, USA
Rocket Used:V-2 rocket
Altitude:Approximately 105 km (65 miles)
Camera Developer:Clyde Holliday
Historical Significance:First photograph of Earth from space
