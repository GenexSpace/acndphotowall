SPACE SHUTTLE PROGRAM (1981–2011)

The Space Shuttle Program was a groundbreaking NASA initiative that introduced reusable spacecraft for human spaceflight. Launched first in 1981 with the shuttle Columbia, the program ran for 30 years, with 135 missions conducted using five orbiters: Columbia, Challenger, Discovery, Atlantis, and Endeavour.

The shuttle played a critical role in building the International Space Station (ISS), deploying satellites like the Hubble Space Telescope, and conducting scientific experiments in microgravity. Its unique design featured an orbiter, external fuel tank, and two solid rocket boosters.

Despite two tragic losses — Challenger (1986) and Columbia (2003) — the program demonstrated humanity’s commitment to long-term space exploration and laid the foundation for future spaceflight technologies.

Specifications and Facts:

First Flight:April 12, 1981 (STS-1)
Last Flight:July 8, 2011 (STS-135)
Number of Missions:135
Orbiters:Columbia, Challenger, Discovery, Atlantis, Endeavour
Payload Capacity:24,400 kg (53,600 lbs)
Major Contributions:ISS construction, Hubble deployment, satellite launches
