Explorer 1 – America’s First Satellite

Explorer-I, officially designated Satellite 1958 Alpha, was the first United States Earth satellite launched as part of the nation’s scientific participation in the International Geophysical Year (1957–1958). The satellite was developed under the leadership of Dr. William H. Pickering at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), with its scientific instrumentation—most notably the cosmic ray detector—designed by Dr. James Van Allen of the State University of Iowa. This instrumentation made Explorer-I a scientific pathfinder, carrying tools to measure cosmic rays and space radiation.

Launched on January 31, 1958, from Cape Canaveral, Florida, atop a Juno I rocket (a modified Redstone missile) engineered by Dr. Wernher von Braun’s team at the Army Ballistic Missile Agency (ABMA), Explorer-I achieved an elliptical orbit and remained aloft for over 100 days. Its data led to the discovery of the Van Allen radiation belts, one of the most significant findings of the space age. Explorer-I marked the United States' formal entry into space exploration and established the credibility of American scientific and engineering capability in the early space race.

Specifications:

Launch Date:January 31, 1958
Launch Vehicle:Juno I
Mass:13.37 kg
Orbit:Low Earth Orbit (LEO)
