GSLV – Powering Heavy Payloads into Space

The Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle (GSLV) is India’s premier launch system for deploying communication and Earth observation satellites into Geostationary Transfer Orbit (GTO). First launched in 2001, GSLV has evolved through multiple versions, including the powerful GSLV Mk III, which is capable of delivering payloads up to 4,000 kg to GTO and 10,000 kg to Low Earth Orbit (LEO).

This vehicle employs a combination of solid, liquid, and cryogenic stages. GSLV Mk III was notably used to launch Chandrayaan-2 and is designated as the launch vehicle for the upcoming Gaganyaan human spaceflight mission. Its development represents a key milestone in India’s capability to independently launch heavier and more complex satellites.

Specifications:

Family:Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle (GSLV)
GSLV Mk I:Used Russian cryogenic upper stage; 2 flights (2001–2003)
GSLV Mk II:Indigenous cryogenic stage (CE-7.5); Payload to GTO: ~2,500 kg
GSLV Mk III (LVM3):India’s heaviest rocket; Payload to GTO: ~4,000 kg; to LEO: ~10,000 kg
First GSLV Launch:April 18, 2001 (Mk I)
First GSLV Mk III Launch:December 18, 2014 (suborbital), full orbital: June 5, 2017
Stages:3 (Solid, Liquid, Cryogenic)
Height (Mk III):43.43 meters
Significant Payloads:Chandrayaan-2, GSAT series, Gaganyaan (planned)
Status:Mk II and Mk III operational; Mk I retired
